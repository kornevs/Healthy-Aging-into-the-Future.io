The care partner intervention (informal caregivers of persons with dementia living at home)

Music–based interventions have been shown to influence levels of hormones, affect the autonomic nervous system and neurotransmitters, help sustain cognition, and to mitigate anxiety, depression, and difficult behaviours associated with dementia. Even low-tech, non-tailored music interventions have a positive impact, but may not be scalable. Even though technology is one of the fastest-growing and most effective mediums for delivering large-scale interventions across settings (e.g., homecare), there is a lack of knowledge about bridging technology with evidence-based outcomes from analogue interventions. Moreover, there is a lack of systematically involving end-users in not only the experience of technology but the goodness of fit of technology in everyday life.

The research question addressed in project C is: How and what technologies (e.g., online music program, digital app, virtual reality) can be beneficial for older adults with frailty including dementia, and their care partners?

Study design and research setting

Care partners (i.e., a spouse, partner, adult child, or friend) are an essential in support of older adults with frailty living at home. Being able to grow older with dignity at home is dependent not only on professional services but on care partners and well-integrated welfare technology. Subsequently, strategies to promote and maintain the health and wellness of care partners is a critical area that requires non-invasive, low-cost, culturally tailored, and broadly generalizable interventions. This project will capitalise on lessons learned from prior research while identifying where we need to go to optimise the use of established and emerging technologies to reduce stress for care partners. Moreover, we recognize that there are unique circumstances in the burden of care for care partners. However, challenges associated with being a care partner for frail older adults with dementia comes with several challenges that require particular attention. To be effective, any technology selected for use in the context of dementia care must possess a unique combination of attributes beyond what might be needed for general medical conditions without impact on cognition. Therefore, this project will also strategically include partner dyads where one person is living with dementia.

Intervention and implementation of the program

The project will utilize a three-step iterative co-creation design with multiple stakeholder groups. In the first step, a database of participants from a prior study will be used to recruit partner dyads where one partner is living with dementia at home. In step two, care partner dyads will be recruited from projects A and B. In the third step, participants from step 1-2 as well as snowball recruitment of other stakeholders will be used. The research setting will constitute different parts of the greater Stockholm area. Sessions can be organized at any our partner organisations to offer sites close to the participants homes.

Methods of data collection and analysis

In the first step, group interviews will be used to explore experiences of a music intervention that was conducted with persons living with dementia at home and their care partners. Interviews will focus on experiences of participating in the music intervention, the degree to which participants continued to use the intervention resources after the intervention ended, and any personally experienced strengths/weaknesses/future wishes relating to the music intervention. The total number of potential participants includes 32 dyads who participated in the music intervention.

In the second step, co-creation workshops will be utilised to engage older adults receiving homecare and their care partners in a generative data process to explore different types of welfare technologies viable for delivering a music-based intervention. A combination of exposure to technologies, trying technologies, group interviews, visualisation, and design thinking methods will be utilised to generate data with focus on critical elements that can inform the potential utility of 1) technology in the delivery of the intervention, 2) technology as accessible, 3) technology as effective, and 4) need for support in the use of technology. Fieldnotes will be used, and interviews and group sessions will be audio recorded.

In the third step, co-creation workshops will be utilized in which stakeholders with various backgrounds will be invited to discuss and prioritize alternatives raised during steps 1-2. It is anticipated that some services and technologies will receive low priority from many participants and some services and technologies will receive high priority from different groupings. This diversity will provide the impetus for further exploration, design, and prototyping.
