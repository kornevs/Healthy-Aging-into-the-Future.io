“All things co” – Stakeholder engagement in intervention design

The project cuts across projects , and and builds on the idea that stakeholder engagement is imperative in designing and evaluating interventions. Without teasing out historical and nuances in terminology, we borrow from the conceptual thinking, "all things co" and used the term co-creation for consistency (Pérez Jolles ´et al 2022). The project integrates co-creation methods to collaborate with stakeholders (i.e., older adults, significant others, care partners relevant in care, healthcare staff, homecare staff, care managers/social workers and management/policy level representatives) to improve user experience, enhance usability, delivery, and strategies to carry out the study design. The reason why this project is included as a separate project is because there is a lack of knowledge about how co-creation can be used and what strategies are most useful in addressing needs within intervention research involving older adults. Moreover, the project is an explicit attempt to ameliorate the risk for silos when large projects launch without intersecting streams in which data gathering and analyses are coordinated across projects with all relevant stakeholders.

The research question addressed in the project is: How can co-creation methods be used to improve user experience and enhance usability, delivery, and strategies relevant for different phases of implementing interventions for older adults living with frailty?

Study design and research setting

Methodologically, the project is grounded in co-creation, which has been shown relevant in several research contexts across healthcare. The project will use an innovative combination of reference groups on an overarching level and visual/qualitative interview methods in the field to generate data together with stakeholders that contributes to identifying priorities, actively work with intervention design, and plan delivery and outcomes. In this program, interventions span from healthcare regulated systems in project to municipality regulated systems in projects and as well as non-regulated community care provided by family in project . Therefore, there will be different types of stakeholders that are of relevance and different parts of the interventions will be in focus. For instance, project commences with a literature review and workshops to integrate knowledge/experiences from two separate trials while project commences with available interview and process evaluation material from an earlier intervention study. Outcomes from co-creation can be intervention materials to be used with older adults, education materials to train staff, or delivery methods of an intervention to meet the needs of care partners.

Methods of data collection and analysis

Sampling will be purposive and constitute older adults and all relevant stakeholder groups. Recruitment for each project will be grounded in the unique project needs. Measures will be taken to ensure that relevant characteristics are represented, such as sex, living conditions, and educational level. In the first step, available data from prior projects, review of prior evidence from the literature, and stakeholder consultations will be carried out. In the second step, co-creation methods including a combination of groupwork, interviews, observations, visualisation, and dialogue will be utilised to enhance, redesign, or innovate intervention components that are prioritized. In step 3, intervention components and training materials will be iteratively prototyped. Examples of prototype outputs include: 1) digital learning platforms, 2) learning materials such as slide decks, brief instruction manuals, and pedagogic guides, 3) exercises of relevance for the intervention, and 4) service delivery processes. Data analysis will be conducted in a two-step process and include both thematic and hermeneutic analyses.
